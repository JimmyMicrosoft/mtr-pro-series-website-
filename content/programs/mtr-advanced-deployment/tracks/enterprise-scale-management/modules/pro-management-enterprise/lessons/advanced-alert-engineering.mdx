## Overview

A 100-room MTR fleet with default alert settings can generate more than 500 incidents per month. Most of those incidents are noise — transient peripheral warnings that self-resolve, brief network blips that last seconds, or duplicate alerts from a single underlying cause. When support teams are overwhelmed by alert volume, they stop engaging with the alert feed entirely: Critical incidents get missed, SLAs are breached, and user complaints become the primary incident detection mechanism. This lesson covers the principles and mechanics of alert engineering in Pro Management — designing rules that surface actionable signals, suppress known noise, correlate related events, and escalate intelligently when acknowledgment does not follow.

---

## The Alert Fatigue Problem at Scale

Alert fatigue is not a perception problem — it is a measurable operational failure. When a support team receives more alerts than they can reasonably process, they develop one of two failure modes:

1. **Triage paralysis:** the team tries to review every alert and falls behind, spending all available time on the alert feed rather than resolutions
2. **Learned dismissal:** the team begins dismissing alerts by default, which means genuinely critical incidents go unacknowledged

**Calculating your alert noise ratio:**

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Alert volume | Total alerts generated per month per 100 rooms | &lt;150 alerts requiring review |
| Action rate | % of alerts that required a field or remote action | >40% |
| Auto-resolve rate | % of alerts that closed without any action | Should not exceed 30% for Critical |
| Acknowledgment within SLA | % of Critical alerts acknowledged within 30 min | >95% |
| Mean time to acknowledge (MTTA) | Average time from alert creation to first acknowledgment | Critical: &lt;15 min |

An action rate below 20% is a strong indicator of alert fatigue — more than 80% of alerts are noise and should be suppressed, filtered, or reclassified.

**Root causes of high alert volume in large MTR deployments:**

- **Over-alerting on Warning severity:** default alert rules often fire Warning-level alerts for transient events (brief peripheral drop, brief app restart) that resolve within minutes and require no action
- **No suppression windows:** alerts fire during scheduled maintenance, overnight, and during known network maintenance periods
- **Peripheral sensitivity too high:** some Pro Management peripheral health checks alert on brief disconnection events (USB re-enumeration on MTR app restart is normal and fires peripheral alerts without a hardware problem)
- **No alert correlation:** three rooms in the same building going offline simultaneously creates three separate Critical incidents when the probable cause is one network event

## Alert Rule Design Principles

A well-designed alert rule set routes the right alert to the right person at the right time. Every alert rule should be justified by answering three questions:
1. **Who needs to know?** (scope: all rooms, specific building, specific room type)
2. **What action does this trigger?** (escalate to on-call, create ITSM ticket, send notification, no action required)
3. **How quickly must they respond?** (which determines severity classification)

**Routing matrix — alerts by severity and type:**

| Alert Type | Severity | Route To | Action |
|-----------|---------|----------|--------|
| Room offline >15 min | Critical | ITSM ticket + on-call page | Field dispatch or remote reboot |
| Camera offline, room in use | Important | ITSM ticket + team channel | Remote troubleshoot, may dispatch |
| Microphone offline, room in use | Important | ITSM ticket + team channel | Remote troubleshoot, may dispatch |
| Display offline | Important | ITSM ticket | Remote troubleshoot |
| Room offline &lt;15 min, auto-resolved | Warning | Log only (no notification) | Review in weekly report |
| Software update available | Warning | Email digest (daily) | Schedule update window |
| Teams app restart (single) | Warning | Log only | Review if recurring |
| Teams app crash (3+ in 1 hour) | Important | ITSM ticket | Investigate app stability |
| Peripheral warning, room not in use | Warning | Log only | Review in weekly report |

<Callout type="tip">
"Log only" alert rules are valuable even without notifications. They create a historical record in Pro Management's incident history that enables root cause analysis and trend reporting. Disable alert rules entirely only for events that generate no diagnostic value whatsoever.
</Callout>

**Scoping alert rules to relevant rooms:**

Not every alert rule should apply to every room. Scoping rules reduce false positive volume:

- **`room-type:focus` and `room-type:huddle` rooms:** these smaller rooms typically have simpler AV setups; apply less aggressive peripheral alerting since they often have basic USB peripherals with higher transient disconnect rates
- **`room-type:boardroom` rooms:** apply stricter alerting thresholds and faster escalation — these rooms have high executive visibility and lower tolerance for impaired functionality
- **Rooms under renovation or decommission:** apply a long-running suppression window rather than generating incidents for rooms known to be offline

## Suppression Windows

Suppression windows (also called maintenance windows) prevent alerts from firing during periods when alerts are expected and not actionable. Pro Management supports scheduled suppression at the building and room level.

**When to use suppression windows:**

**Scheduled maintenance:** When performing firmware updates, AV reconfiguration, or network maintenance that will take rooms offline, create a suppression window for the affected rooms or building for the duration of the maintenance. Without suppression, a 2-hour maintenance window for 20 rooms generates 20+ Critical "room offline" incidents that all auto-resolve — polluting the incident history and wasting alert review time.

**After-hours for low-priority alerts:** Configure Warning-level alerts to suppress outside business hours (e.g., 18:00–07:00 local building time). A peripheral warning at 02:00 that resolves by 08:00 when staff arrive does not benefit from an overnight notification.

**During known infrastructure events:** When your network team notifies you of a WAN circuit maintenance window, proactively create a suppression for all rooms in the affected building. This is better than receiving 30 simultaneous Critical alerts and having to manually close them post-maintenance.

**Suppression window configuration:**

Navigate to: Pro Management → Settings → Maintenance windows → Add

```
Name:         "NYC-HQ Network Maintenance Apr 14"
Buildings:    AMER — New York — HQ
Start:        2026-04-14 22:00 (building local time)
End:          2026-04-15 02:00 (building local time)
Alert types:  All (suppress all alerts)
Notes:        "Cisco switch firmware upgrade — WAN circuit offline"
```

<Callout type="warning">
Suppression windows in Pro Management suppress alert notifications and ITSM webhook delivery. The underlying health events are still recorded in room history. After the suppression window ends, verify that rooms have returned to healthy status — suppression does not auto-resolve the health events, it only stops them from generating notifications during the window.
</Callout>

## Correlating Related Alerts

The most valuable — and most manually intensive — alert analysis practice is identifying when multiple separate alerts share a common root cause. Three rooms in the same building going offline within 60 seconds of each other is almost never three independent device failures; it is almost certainly one network event (switch failure, VLAN misconfiguration, DHCP scope exhaustion).

**Recognizing correlated alert patterns:**

| Pattern | Probable Cause | Escalation |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| 3+ rooms in same building offline simultaneously | Network switch/VLAN failure, DHCP issue | Network team, not AV team |
| All rooms on same floor offline | Floor switch failure or floor VLAN issue | Network team, floor switch investigation |
| All rooms in same building healthy→degraded→healthy within 30 min | Transient network blip or building power issue | Review building network logs; no dispatch if resolved |
| Same peripheral type offline across multiple rooms in same building | Potential firmware issue with peripheral; USB driver update may have caused issue | Check if recent update was applied; coordinate with AV vendor |
| Rooms added to same Intune update ring rebooting unexpectedly | Windows update completed forced restart during business hours | Review update ring active hours configuration |

**Manual correlation workflow (current Pro Management capability):**

Pro Management does not natively correlate alerts or suppress related incidents as a single event. Correlation is currently a manual analytical step:

1. When reviewing the incident feed, observe multiple simultaneous Critical incidents
2. Sort by timestamp — if 5+ rooms fired within a 2-minute window, investigate network before dispatching to rooms
3. Look for the building/floor/switch scope using the building and floor tags to identify the common factor
4. Open a single ITSM "parent" incident for the network event and link the room-level incidents as children

<Callout type="tip">
If your ITSM integration is active, instruct the ITSM deduplication logic (from Lesson 2) to pause new ticket creation for 5 minutes after the first ticket from a given building is created. This provides a short correlation window to assess whether a pattern is forming before creating 15 separate P1 tickets.
</Callout>

## Escalation Chains

An escalation chain defines what happens when an acknowledged action does not occur within the expected time. Without escalation chains, a Critical alert can sit unacknowledged for hours during a shift handover, a weekend, or when the primary on-call technician is unavailable.

**Escalation chain design:**

For a Critical alert, a three-tier chain is appropriate for most enterprise deployments:

```
Tier 1 — Immediate notification (T+0)
  → ITSM ticket created (webhook)
  → Teams channel notification (alert rule webhook to Teams)
  → On-call technician SMS/push notification

Tier 2 — Acknowledgment SLA breach (T+30 min, if not acknowledged)
  → Escalate to regional AV lead
  → Add escalation note to ITSM ticket
  → Send Teams message to escalation group

Tier 3 — Resolution SLA breach (T+4 hours, if not resolved)
  → Escalate to IT management
  → Trigger management bridge call notification
  → Flag in executive status dashboard
```

**Implementing escalation in Pro Management + ITSM:**

Pro Management's native alert rules support notification delivery (webhook, email) but do not have a built-in timer-based escalation mechanism. Escalation chains are implemented by combining Pro Management's alert delivery with ITSM escalation rules:

- **ServiceNow:** configure SLA definitions on the incident table for P1 incidents; set SLA escalation actions at 30-minute (if not acknowledged) and 4-hour (if not resolved) thresholds
- **Jira Service Management:** configure SLA policies with Time to First Response and Time to Resolution goals; set up Jira Automation escalation actions when SLA is breached
- **Power Automate:** for organizations without full ITSM SLA tooling, a Power Automate scheduled flow can query open Pro Management incidents via webhook state and send escalation Teams messages if incidents older than 30 minutes remain unacknowledged

## Alert Effectiveness Metrics and Tuning

Building a good alert rule set is not a one-time activity. Alert rules require tuning as the fleet matures, as infrastructure changes, and as the support team's operational patterns evolve. The foundation of tuning is measurement.

**Monthly alert review metrics (pull from Pro Management + ITSM):**

| Metric | How to Measure | Tuning Action If Off-Target |
|--------|---------------|---------------------------|
| Alert volume per 100 rooms | Count incidents in Pro Management for the month | >300/month: review Warning rules; reduce peripheral sensitivity |
| Action rate | ITSM tickets closed with "resolved by action" / total tickets | &lt;30%: too many auto-resolving alerts being sent to ITSM |
| Acknowledgment rate within 30 min | % of Critical alerts acknowledged within 30 min | &lt;90%: review on-call notification delivery; check escalation chain |
| Noise alert rate | Alerts auto-resolved within 15 min with no ITSM ticket | >20% of total: apply time-delay to alert firing (only fire if condition persists >15 min) |
| Suppression effectiveness | Alerts fired during active suppression windows | Any: suppression windows not applied correctly; audit configuration |

**30-day tuning process:**

At the end of each month, run the following analysis against the previous 30 days of incident data:

1. **Export all incidents** from Pro Management (Analytics → Export)
2. **Sort by alert type:** identify the top 5 alert types by volume
3. **For each high-volume alert type:** calculate what percentage resulted in a technician action
4. **Action rate &lt;20%:** consider either raising the threshold for this alert type (e.g., require condition to persist for 20 minutes before firing) or reclassifying it to Warning/log-only
5. **Review all "Warning" alerts:** if any Warning alert has never triggered an action in 30 days, evaluate whether it provides value and consider disabling

**Tuning log:** maintain a change log of alert rule modifications with date, rationale, and expected impact. This enables you to correlate changes in alert volume/quality to specific rule changes and revert modifications that produce unintended results.

## Summary

- **Alert fatigue** is measurable: track alert volume per 100 rooms, action rate, auto-resolve rate, and acknowledgment within SLA; an action rate below 20% signals excessive noise.
- **Alert rule design:** route by severity × type × scope; route Critical room-offline events to ITSM; route Warning-level transient events to log-only.
- **Suppression windows:** apply for scheduled maintenance, after-hours Warning suppression, and proactive infrastructure event suppression to keep incident history clean.
- **Alert correlation:** identify simultaneous multi-room events as probable network/infrastructure causes; use building + floor tags to scope investigation; link ITSM incidents as parent/child.
- **Escalation chains:** combine Pro Management webhook delivery with ITSM SLA rules for timer-based escalation (T+30 min acknowledgment, T+4h resolution).
- **Monthly tuning:** apply time-delay thresholds to high-volume auto-resolving alert types; maintain a change log; target action rate >40%.
