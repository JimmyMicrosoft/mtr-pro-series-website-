## Overview

Every MTR project starts with a bill of materials. Before you can write a BOM you can stand behind — one you can quote, order, and deliver without surprises — you need to understand what goes into an MTR system, what Microsoft's certification program actually means, and how to use the certified device catalog to find compliant components for any room. This lesson builds that foundation.

The hardware knowledge covered here is not academic. It translates directly into the decisions you make on every project: which compute unit to specify, which camera covers a given room size, which microphone array handles the table length, whether the client's proposed display will work correctly with MTR. These are the daily decisions of an integrator working in the MTR space, and making them accurately — the first time — is what separates professional deployments from costly change orders.

By the end of this lesson you will know the function of each hardware category in the MTR signal chain, how to navigate the certified device catalog, and how to apply room size guidance to match hardware to the actual spaces you are designing.

## The Certification Program

Microsoft operates a formal hardware certification program for Teams Rooms. The program tests devices — compute units, cameras, audio devices, touch consoles, and content cameras — against a defined set of compatibility requirements with the Teams Rooms application. A device that passes receives a certified designation and appears in the Microsoft Teams Rooms certified devices catalog at aka.ms/MTRDevices.

The practical meaning of certification is this: if you deploy a device that is on the certified list, Microsoft and the device OEM will support it. If a problem arises, Microsoft's support team will engage on your issue. If you deploy a non-certified device, Microsoft's position is that the configuration is unsupported — their support team will not troubleshoot issues related to the non-certified component, and the OEM may decline to support it under warranty in an MTR context. For integrators, specifying non-certified hardware creates a support gap that becomes your problem.

The catalog is organized by category — Teams Rooms on Windows, Teams Rooms on Android, peripherals — and includes filters for room size (small, medium, large), device type (cameras, audio, consoles), and vendor. Room size ratings are Microsoft's guidance, not hard limits, but they reflect testing done with specific room dimensions and seating configurations. A camera rated for small rooms has been validated for coverage in a space of that type; using it in a large room is an unsupported configuration that may produce inadequate coverage.

<Callout type="warning">
  Always verify that the devices you specify are on the current certified list at the time you
  write your proposal. The catalog is updated regularly — new devices are added, older models
  reach end-of-support, and room size ratings can change with firmware updates. A device that
  was certified when you wrote the quote may have been superseded by the time you order. Build
  a catalog verification step into your pre-proposal workflow.
</Callout>

## Compute Units and Touch Consoles

The compute unit is the central component of an MTR Windows system. It runs Windows IoT Enterprise, runs the Teams Rooms application, and connects to every other device in the room via USB or IP. Every signal in the room flows through it: camera video, microphone audio, display output, content sharing from HDMI input, touch console commands. Its processing power, port count, and form factor determine what you can build around it.

Representative compute units include the Lenovo ThinkSmart Core (a compact unit designed to mount in AV furniture or behind a display), the Crestron UC-MX70-T (a rack-mountable solution that integrates with Crestron's AV control ecosystem), and the HP Presence Compute Module. Some integrators build around Intel NUC-based platforms paired with controllers like the Logitech Tap IP. When selecting a compute unit, consider: how many USB 3.0 ports are available for peripherals, how many display outputs are needed (HDMI or DisplayPort), whether an IP-based or USB-based touch console is appropriate for the room's layout, and whether the form factor fits the furniture or rack space available.

The touch console — the controller — is the room's user interface. It is the 8" or 10" touchscreen device that sits on the conference table and shows the room's calendar, meeting join buttons, call controls, and settings. On MTR Windows, the console connects to the compute unit via USB (for same-room installations with short cable runs) or via Ethernet (for IP-based consoles, which remove cable length constraints and allow the compute unit to be located in a remote rack). On MTR Android, the situation differs: most Android all-in-one devices include a companion touch controller that pairs wirelessly or via a short IP connection, and both devices run coordinated instances of the Teams Rooms app.

## Displays

Display selection for MTR rooms is not simply a matter of screen size. The displays in a conference room operate for eight to twelve hours a day, five days a week, in a professional environment where image quality and reliability are expected. That is a very different duty cycle than a home television — and it is the primary reason commercial-grade displays are the appropriate specification for MTR rooms.

Commercial displays are rated for extended daily operation (typically 16/7 or 24/7 duty cycles), maintain brightness calibration more consistently over time, often include RS-232 or LAN control interfaces for integration with room control systems, and carry commercial warranties that cover the use case. Consumer televisions are designed for home use — a few hours per evening — and are neither warranted nor optimized for the demands of a commercial conference room. MTR uses CEC (Consumer Electronics Control) to send wake and sleep commands to displays when meetings start and end. CEC support varies across consumer panels and is more reliable and consistently implemented on commercial-grade displays.

Brightness matters more than most integrators account for early in their careers. The standard recommendation is a minimum of 300 nits for a typical conference room with controlled lighting and window blinds. Rooms with significant ambient light — particularly those with south-facing windows or limited shade coverage — benefit from 500 nits or higher. Undersized brightness means the display is visually dominated by ambient light, reducing readability of shared content and video for in-room participants.

Sizing follows a straightforward rule of thumb: the viewing distance should not exceed 1.5 to 2 times the display diagonal. A room with a 20-foot viewing distance calls for at least a 120" image equivalent — achievable with an 85" display at closer range or a projection system for the full depth. For standard boardrooms with 15-foot viewing distances, 75" to 85" displays are appropriate for single-display configurations.

MTR Windows supports up to two displays. The second display typically shows the content being shared in the meeting — a remote participant's screen share or the local HDMI input from a laptop — while the first display shows people video. The display assignment (which is primary, which is secondary) is configurable in the MTR XML settings. For rooms designed with dual displays, this must be specified and configured during commissioning.

<Callout type="tip">
  When clients ask to use consumer televisions to save cost, the right response is to explain
  the duty cycle difference, note the warranty gap, and document their choice in writing if they
  insist. Clients who save $400 on a TV and call for support six months later because the panel
  is degrading are your ongoing support problem. The commercial display specification is in
  their interest, not just yours.
</Callout>

## Cameras

Camera selection is driven by room geometry — specifically, the number of seats, the table configuration, and the distance from the camera to the farthest participant. Microsoft's certified camera catalog covers devices from wide-angle fixed cameras appropriate for small rooms to motorized PTZ cameras with tracking capabilities for large spaces.

For small rooms (up to six seats, typical huddle or small conference configuration), fixed wide-angle cameras with a 90-degree or wider field of view provide adequate coverage without mechanical complexity. Devices like the Logitech Rally Bar Mini (with integrated camera and audio), the Jabra PanaCast 20, or the Poly Studio P15 cover these rooms with a single device. The camera field of view at close range captures all participants without PTZ movement.

For medium rooms (six to twelve seats, standard boardroom configuration), smart-tracking cameras that follow the active speaker or provide a wide-field persistent view are appropriate. The Poly Studio E70 uses AI-based framing to automatically track the active speaker or provide a group shot. The Huddly IQ provides wide-field intelligent framing. The Logitech MX Brio and similar 4K cameras offer high resolution for rooms where image quality is a priority. These rooms benefit from cameras positioned at the front wall, centered on the display, at approximately screen height.

For large rooms (twelve or more seats, training rooms, or executive conference rooms with complex geometry), PTZ cameras with preset positions become necessary. The Sony SRG-A12, the Aver CAM540, and similar PTZ units can track speakers, switch between preset views (full table, presenter area, audience view), and provide the flexibility that large-room coverage requires. These rooms may also use multiple cameras — one at the front covering the presenter and display area, one at the rear covering the far end of the table.

## Audio Devices and Content Cameras

Audio is the most technically demanding aspect of MTR room design and the most common source of post-installation issues. A room can have a perfect picture and still be unusable if remote participants cannot hear clearly. The core variables are microphone coverage area (does the pickup pattern reach every seat at the table), echo cancellation quality (does the device adequately suppress the room's speaker output from the microphone feed), and noise rejection (does the device handle HVAC noise, paper shuffling, and room reflections without degrading speech intelligibility).

For small rooms, integrated audio bars — devices that combine camera, microphone array, and speaker in one unit — simplify the design and typically provide adequate coverage for up to six seats. The Jabra PanaCast 50, the Poly Studio R30, and the Yealink UVC40 represent this category. The integrated designs are tested and certified as a complete audio/video solution, which simplifies the certification question.

For medium rooms, dedicated tabletop microphone arrays or ceiling-mounted microphones become appropriate as the table length grows beyond what an integrated bar can cover reliably. Devices like the Shure MXA310 (tabletop array) or the EPOS EXPAND 80T provide focused pickup across a conference table. Ceiling microphone arrays — the Shure MXA910, Biamp Tesira Forte with ceiling array, or Q-SYS-based solutions — become the right architecture for rooms where a clean table surface without microphone devices is a design requirement or where the table is too long for a single tabletop array.

For large rooms, the audio design moves into DSP territory. A ceiling microphone array covering the full table, a DSP processor handling echo cancellation and mixing, and a distributed ceiling or line array speaker system are standard. The MTR compute unit connects to the DSP via USB (using a USB audio interface) or via a certified USB-to-DSP bridge device. Acoustic design — calculating microphone coverage area against table dimensions and seating positions, specifying speaker distribution for even SPL across the room — is a discipline in its own right and is covered in depth in later tracks.

<Callout type="info">
  Matching microphone coverage area to the physical table is a calculation, not an estimate.
  A microphone array with a 20-foot diameter coverage radius does not adequately cover a 30-foot
  conference table. Calculate coverage before specifying. For tables longer than a microphone's
  rated coverage area, specify supplemental pickup devices or move to a ceiling array
  architecture that provides zone-based coverage.
</Callout>

Content cameras are a secondary category relevant to rooms where physical whiteboard use is regular and remote participants need to see whiteboard content clearly. A content camera is a USB camera — separate from the main meeting camera — pointed at the whiteboard. MTR Windows includes AI processing that digitizes the whiteboard image, enhances contrast and readability, and optionally removes the presenter standing in front of the board. Correct positioning is specific: the camera must be perpendicular to the whiteboard surface, positioned 3 to 12 feet away, with the entire whiteboard surface in frame. Content cameras are only effective when the room design accounts for their position from the start.

## Building a Bill of Materials

The BOM exercise that follows uses a standard medium conference room as the working scenario: a 10-seat room, single display, standard boardroom lighting, no whiteboard requirement. The goal is to walk through each hardware category in sequence — compute, console, display, camera, audio — selecting a certified component for each and validating that the combination is a supported configuration.

Before building any BOM, confirm three things: all components are on the current certified list, the combination of components is a supported configuration (some compute units have USB port limitations that restrict certain peripheral combinations), and the room's physical constraints (cable distances, mounting requirements, furniture type) are compatible with the selected hardware. Component-level certification does not automatically validate a full system combination — verify the system as a configuration, not just as individual parts.

Some manufacturers offer pre-certified bundles — the Lenovo ThinkSmart Bundle, the Logitech Room Solution for Medium Rooms, the Poly Studio Room Kit — that package compute, console, camera, and audio as a validated system. Bundles simplify the certification question for standard room types and can reduce BOM complexity on straightforward deployments. They are not always the best value or the best fit for rooms with specific requirements, but they are a legitimate starting point for common room sizes.

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **Navigate the certified device catalog:** Access aka.ms/MTRDevices, filter by category and room size, and verify device certification status before specifying any component.
- **Describe the MTR Windows signal chain:** Compute unit at center, connected to touch console (USB or IP), displays (HDMI/DP), camera (USB 3.0), audio device (USB), and network (Ethernet).
- **Select hardware by room size:** Small rooms — integrated bars for camera and audio, fixed wide-angle cameras. Medium rooms — dedicated microphone arrays, smart-tracking cameras. Large rooms — ceiling microphone arrays or DSP-based audio, PTZ cameras with presets.
- **Specify displays correctly:** Commercial-grade panels for duty cycle and warranty coverage. Minimum 300 nits brightness. Appropriately sized for viewing distance. CEC for auto wake/sleep.
- **Understand content cameras:** Secondary USB camera for whiteboard capture, Windows-only AI processing, specific positioning requirements (3–12 feet, perpendicular, full whiteboard in frame).
