## Overview

Not all Teams meetings are the same, and that distinction matters more than most integrators realize when they are designing and configuring rooms. A private meeting, a channel meeting, a webinar, and a town hall each behave differently in terms of how the room joins, who controls the experience, what the AV requirements look like, and what can go wrong. Knowing these differences prevents the kind of support call where a client reports that "the room doesn't show meetings" — when the actual cause is that the resource account was never added to the Teams channel where the meeting was scheduled.

This lesson covers the four primary meeting types, how they interact with MTR rooms specifically, and the AV design implications that arise when a client's use case goes beyond a standard private meeting. It is a shorter lesson but a foundational one — the meeting type context will come up repeatedly in later modules on configuration, troubleshooting, and client consulting.

---

## Private Meetings

A private meeting is the most common meeting type in enterprise Teams use. It is scheduled via Outlook or the Teams calendar application, sent to specific invited attendees by email address, and appears on those attendees' calendars. When the room resource account is invited to a private meeting, the meeting appears on the room's MTR touch console as a scheduled event with a one-touch join button. This is the join flow that most clients associate with "how the room works" and the one that the MTR platform is primarily optimized for.

From an AV perspective, private meetings are straightforward. All meeting participants have presenter rights by default unless the organizer modifies meeting options. Content sharing via HDMI input and screen share is fully supported. The full MTR feature set — camera, audio, display, content sharing, layout selection — is available. Post-installation testing should always use a private meeting as the baseline functional test before testing other scenarios.

<Callout type="note">
  When a private meeting invite includes the room resource account, the meeting appears on
  the touch console approximately 15 minutes before the scheduled start time. If meetings are
  not appearing, the first diagnostic step is to confirm the resource account was actually
  included in the meeting invite and that Exchange Online AutomateProcessing is set to
  AutoAccept. These two conditions are responsible for the vast majority of "no meetings
  showing" support calls.
</Callout>

## Channel Meetings

A channel meeting is scheduled within a specific Teams channel — a structured group communication space within a Teams team. Any member of that channel can join the meeting without a direct invite. Channel meetings appear in the Teams client's channel activity feed rather than (or in addition to) the organizer's calendar.

The AV implication for integrators is a specific and common support scenario: the room resource account must be a member of the Teams team (and specifically have access to the channel) for the channel meeting to appear on the room's touch console. If the IT team created the resource account correctly and the room joins private meetings without issue, but does not show channel meetings, the first thing to check is whether the resource account has been added to the relevant Teams team. This is a configuration item for the IT administrator, not a hardware or network issue, but it is the integrator's job to know what to ask.

The resource account does not need to be explicitly invited to each channel meeting — it only needs to be a team member for channel meetings in that team to propagate to the room's calendar. Once added, any future channel meeting in that team will appear on the room's console automatically.

## Webinars

A webinar is a structured Teams event designed for a defined presenter-attendee model. It includes formal registration, controlled attendee management, and a clear distinction between presenters (who can share content, speak, and appear on camera) and attendees (who are muted by default and have limited interaction capabilities beyond reactions and Q&A). When an MTR room joins a webinar as a room — not as a named presenter — it joins as an attendee by default, which means its microphone is muted and it cannot share content unless explicitly promoted to presenter by the webinar organizer.

The AV implications depend on the room's role in the webinar. Rooms used purely for attendee viewing — a team watching a training webinar together from a conference room — have minimal special requirements beyond a good display and audio system for the in-room audience to hear the webinar content clearly. Rooms used as presenter spaces — where the webinar host or panelists are presenting from a physical room — require higher production quality: a professional camera framing the presenter correctly, a clean and quiet audio environment, good lighting, and content sharing that is reliable enough for a live broadcast context. These are meaningfully different AV specifications, and integrators should ask clients how webinars will be used before finalizing hardware selections.

<Callout type="tip">
  When a client mentions webinar use as a requirement, ask specifically: is the room being
  used as a presenter space or an attendee space? The answer changes the camera, audio, and
  lighting specifications significantly. A presenter webinar room warrants a higher-quality
  camera with clean framing, acoustic treatment considerations, and broadcast-quality audio.
  An attendee viewing room just needs a good screen and speakers.
</Callout>

## Town Halls and Large-Scale Events

Town halls — the feature Microsoft rebranded from "Live Events" — are large-scale broadcast experiences supporting up to 10,000 attendees. They have a formal production model: designated producers manage the broadcast, presenters are in a green room or dedicated production space, and attendees watch a one-way video stream with limited interaction capabilities (Q&A, reactions). Town halls are not standard MTR meeting join scenarios — the production room for a town hall typically requires broadcast-grade infrastructure that goes well beyond a standard Teams Rooms configuration.

For integrators, town hall production rooms are a specialized scope that may involve external encoders, professional camera switching, vision mixers, professional audio consoles, teleprompters, and lighting rigs. The MTR device in a town hall production room, if present at all, is typically used as a monitoring endpoint or for green room preview — not as the primary production source. If a client mentions town halls as a requirement, clarify whether they need a dedicated production room and scope it separately from standard MTR conference rooms.

The practical takeaway for most integrators: private meetings and channel meetings represent the overwhelming majority of room use cases. Webinars require additional scoping questions. Town halls are an entirely different engagement. Document the meeting type requirements explicitly in your pre-sale discovery and scope accordingly.

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **Describe the four meeting types:** Private meetings (direct invite, full MTR feature set), channel meetings (open to team members, resource account must be a team member), webinars (presenter/attendee model, room joins as attendee by default), and town halls (large-scale broadcast, production-room scope).
- **Explain the channel meeting gap:** The most common "room shows no meetings" issue for channel meetings is a resource account that was not added to the Teams team. This is an IT configuration item, not a hardware problem.
- **Scope webinar and town hall rooms differently:** Webinar presenter rooms warrant higher AV production quality; town hall production rooms are an entirely different engagement scope.
