## Overview

Specifying the wrong cable type is a persistent source of installation problems. The cable arrives on-site, gets installed in conduit or ceiling pathways, and the signal fails — either immediately or intermittently. Tracing an intermittent problem to a cable specification error after the walls are closed is expensive. Getting the cable specification right before the BOM is finalized prevents the problem entirely.

This lesson covers every cable type used in MTR signal paths: what differentiates the variants, what the performance implications are, and how to match the right cable to each application.

---

## Network Cables — Cat6 and Cat6A

**Cat6** supports 1 Gbps at the full 100-meter channel length and 10 Gbps at approximately 55 meters. Its conductors are typically 24 AWG and the cable geometry manages near-end crosstalk within TIA-568 specifications for 1G applications.

**Cat6A** (Category 6 Augmented) supports 10 Gbps at the full 100-meter channel length. It achieves this through tighter geometry specifications, typically larger 23 AWG conductors, enhanced shielding in some variants (F/UTP or S/FTP designs), and a physically larger jacket — Cat6A cables are noticeably bulkier than Cat6. The "augmented" specification specifically addresses alien crosstalk (interference between adjacent cables), which is the limiting factor for 10G performance at distance.

For MTR installations, Cat6A is the recommended baseline for all new network infrastructure. The cost difference between Cat6 and Cat6A is modest at the cable level, and the future-proofing value of 10G-capable infrastructure for a system that will serve a room for 5–10 years is significant. All compute unit network connections and any connections to PoE-powered devices should use Cat6A.

<Callout type="tip">
  Verify that the Cat6A cable you specify is compatible with the keystones, patch panels,
  and plugs in the BOM. Cat6A has a larger cable diameter than Cat6 and requires Cat6A-rated
  jacks and plugs — Cat6 keystones will not achieve Cat6A performance and may not physically
  accept the larger cable properly.
</Callout>

## Video Cables — HDMI 2.0 and HDMI 2.1

**HDMI 2.0** supports up to 4K resolution at 60Hz with a maximum bandwidth of 18 Gbps. It handles 4K/60Hz HDR content and supports up to 32 audio channels. For current MTR deployments, HDMI 2.0 is the standard specification — most MTR compute units output 4K/60Hz, and HDMI 2.0 cables carry that signal reliably.

**HDMI 2.1** supports resolutions up to 8K at 60Hz or 4K at 120Hz with a maximum bandwidth of 48 Gbps. It also introduces Variable Refresh Rate (VRR) and Auto Low Latency Mode (ALLM). For MTR rooms today, HDMI 2.1 capability is not required by current compute hardware but provides meaningful future-proofing.

For new infrastructure installations, specifying HDMI 2.1 cable adds minimal cost and ensures compatibility with next-generation hardware. When specifying HDMI 2.1 cable, use cables with published specifications from commercial AV suppliers — consumer HDMI 2.1 cables vary significantly in quality, and unrated cables may not maintain signal integrity at full 48 Gbps bandwidth in commercial installation lengths.

## USB Cables — Type A, Type C, and Version Matters

USB cables used in MTR systems connect cameras, audio devices, touch consoles (in some configurations), and BYOD connections. The connector type (A or C) matters less than the USB version the cable supports.

**USB 2.0** supports 480 Mbps bandwidth. This is sufficient for audio devices and USB 2.0 cameras. Passive cable limit: 5 meters. Active USB 2.0 cables extend this to approximately 15–20 meters.

**USB 3.x (USB 3.2 Gen 1 and Gen 2)** supports 5 Gbps (Gen 1) or 10 Gbps (Gen 2). USB 3.x is required for cameras that output uncompressed or high-bitrate 4K video. This is critical: an MTR-certified 4K auto-tracking camera connected via a USB 2.0 cable will either fail to initialize or produce degraded video. Always verify the USB version requirement from the camera's technical specification sheet and match the cable accordingly. Passive cable limit for USB 3.x: approximately 2–3 meters. Active USB 3.x cables are required for any run beyond that.

**USB-A vs. USB-C connectors:** USB-C is smaller and symmetrical (reversible). USB-A is the traditional rectangular connector. The connector type is determined by the device ports — match the cable connector to the device connector. Many modern compute units use USB-C for high-speed camera connections and USB-A for legacy peripherals.

## Video Cables — DisplayPort 1.4

DisplayPort 1.4 supports up to 32.4 Gbps bandwidth (HBR3), enabling 8K/60Hz or 4K/120Hz output. It is used on some compute units as the primary display output, particularly when the compute unit is a compact PC with both DisplayPort and HDMI outputs.

Passive DisplayPort cables are reliable up to approximately 3 meters at full HBR3 bandwidth. Active DisplayPort cables or fiber DP (Active Optical Cable) solutions are required for runs beyond 3 meters. DP-to-HDMI adapters are technically viable but introduce potential compatibility and EDID/HDCP issues — where possible, match the display input to the compute output type to avoid adapters entirely. If an adapter is unavoidable, use a passive adapter for short runs and an active adapter for longer runs where signal conditioning is needed.

---

## Audio Cables — Speakon and Euroblock

**Speakon (NL4FC)** connectors are the professional standard for amplified speaker wiring. Speakon provides a locking, high-current-rated connection that is safer and more reliable than bare wire, banana plugs, or spring-clip terminals. They are used in MTR rooms with separate amplifiers and ceiling or surface speakers — typically medium to large rooms where a USB audio bar is insufficient for coverage or volume requirements. The NL4FC designation indicates a 4-pole Speakon connector, which supports dual-channel (stereo) amplifier connections on a single connector.

**Euroblock (Phoenix/pluggable terminal block)** connectors are used for line-level audio I/O, RS-232/485 control, GPIO, and low-current power connections on DSPs, amplifiers, and control systems. Biamp TesiraFORTE, QSC Q-SYS Core, and similar DSP platforms use Euroblock throughout. Correct Euroblock termination technique: strip approximately 5–6mm of insulation, insert fully into the terminal, tighten the set screw to the specified torque. Loose Euroblock connections — the most common Euroblock failure — cause intermittent audio loss or control failures that are difficult to diagnose without a termination inspection.

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **Select network cable:** Cat6A for all new network infrastructure; Cat6 acceptable for short runs and replacements in existing Cat6 systems.
- **Specify video cable:** HDMI 2.0 for current MTR compute; HDMI 2.1 or fiber for new infrastructure; active cables or HDBaseT for runs over 10–15 meters.
- **Specify USB cable:** Match USB version to camera requirements (USB 3.x for 4K cameras); use active cables for any USB 3.x run over 2–3 meters or USB 2.0 run over 5 meters.
- **Identify audio connectors:** Speakon for amplified speaker runs; Euroblock for line-level DSP I/O and control connections.
