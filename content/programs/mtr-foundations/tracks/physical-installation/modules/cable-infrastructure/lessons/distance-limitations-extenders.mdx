## Overview

Every cable type has a passive distance limit — the maximum length at which the signal can travel reliably without amplification or conversion. In conference room installations, these limits are regularly exceeded. A camera mounted above the ceiling tile, a display at the far end of a large boardroom, a compute unit in a rack room rather than in the credenza — each of these scenarios introduces cable runs that may exceed passive limits. The solution for each signal type is different, and selecting the wrong extension approach creates reliability problems that are difficult to diagnose after installation.

This lesson maps the passive limits for each signal type and describes the correct extension technology for each scenario.

---

## Passive Distance Limits

Understanding these limits is foundational — commit them to memory.

**Cat6/6A — Network:** 100 meters maximum channel length for 1 Gbps. Cat6A extends 10 Gbps to the full 100 meters; Cat6 supports 10 Gbps reliably to approximately 55 meters. Beyond 100 meters, a network switch or media converter is required to extend the segment.

**HDMI (passive cable):** Reliable to approximately 10–15 meters for 4K/60Hz (HDMI 2.0 bandwidth). Some high-quality passive cables perform reliably to 15 meters; others show signal degradation at 10 meters. Do not specify passive HDMI for any run you cannot guarantee is under 10 meters.

**HDMI (active cable):** Active HDMI cables incorporate signal conditioning electronics in the connector housings — they are powered from the source device's HDMI port. Active HDMI cables extend reliability to approximately 20–30 meters. They do not require external power but are directional — install with source and display ends in the correct orientation.

**USB 2.0 (passive):** 5 meters. Active USB 2.0 cables (inline repeater) extend this to approximately 15–20 meters.

**USB 3.x (passive):** 2–3 meters for USB 3.2 Gen 1 (5 Gbps) and 1–2 meters for Gen 2 (10 Gbps). Active USB 3.x cables extend to approximately 5–10 meters depending on quality. For longer runs, USB over CAT extenders are required.

**DisplayPort 1.4 (passive):** Approximately 3 meters at HBR3 (32.4 Gbps). Active DP cables or fiber DP (Active Optical Cable) solutions are required for longer runs.

## HDBaseT Extension

HDBaseT is the dominant long-run HDMI extension technology for commercial AV. It converts the HDMI signal to a format that travels over a single Cat6/6A cable run, transmitting video, audio, control (RS-232, IR), and Ethernet up to 100 meters. HDBaseT Class A supports the full 100-meter distance; Class B supports up to 40 meters.

An HDBaseT installation consists of a transmitter (connected to the HDMI source at the near end) and a receiver (connected to the display at the far end), connected by a Cat6A cable. The transmitter and receiver require power — either from the switch's PoE port or from local power supplies, depending on the specific HDBaseT product.

Key implementation considerations:
- Use Cat6A for HDBaseT runs, not Cat6 — Cat6's alien crosstalk performance is insufficient for reliable HDBaseT at full 100-meter distance in dense cable environments
- HDCP compliance must be maintained through the HDBaseT extender chain — verify that the transmitter/receiver pair is HDCP 2.2 compliant for 4K HDR content
- HDBaseT passes RS-232 and IR control signals alongside video — this is a useful feature for rooms with display power control through the compute unit or control system
- The Cat6A cable used for HDBaseT must be a home-run (dedicated) cable — do not use patch through switches or patch panels between the transmitter and receiver

## USB Over CAT Extenders

USB over CAT extenders extend USB signals (2.0 and 3.x) over Cat5e/6 runs using a transmitter/receiver extender pair. This technology is essential for ceiling-mounted cameras — the camera connects to the transmitter at the ceiling, a Cat6A cable runs to the receiver near the compute unit, and the USB connection appears normally to the compute unit.

USB 2.0 over CAT: supports distances of 50–100 meters depending on the product.
USB 3.x over CAT: more demanding — quality products from Icron, Crestron, or Legrand/C2G support 50–100 meters at USB 3.2 Gen 1 speeds, but the product must be verified for compatibility with the specific camera model and compute unit combination.

USB over CAT extenders require local power at the receiver end. USB bus power is insufficient at extended distances — the receiver needs its own power supply. Include power supply requirements in the BOM and plan an outlet or PoE-to-USB power injection at the receiver location.

<Callout type="warning">
  Not all USB over CAT extenders work with all cameras. Verify compatibility between
  the extender pair and the specific camera model before installation. Test the combination
  at the integrator's facility during staging — an incompatible extender/camera pair
  discovered on installation day cannot be resolved without a replacement unit, which
  will halt the installation.
</Callout>

## Fiber Optic HDMI

Fiber optic HDMI cables (also called Active Optical Cables or AOC) carry HDMI signals over optical fiber instead of copper. They support HDMI 2.0 at distances of 50–300 meters with no signal degradation — the signal quality at 200 meters is identical to 1 meter. The cable is thin and flexible (much more so than copper HDMI at comparable distances), making it easy to route through conduit and tight pathways.

Fiber AOC cables are directional (source and display ends are labeled) and do not require external power — they are powered from the HDMI port. They provide ground loop isolation, which eliminates a common source of video noise (60Hz hum or image tearing) in AV systems where the source and display are on different power circuits.

Fiber is the appropriate choice for: display runs over 30 meters, installations in electrically noisy environments (near large motors, VFDs, or power distribution equipment), and any installation where ground loop isolation would be beneficial (multiple power circuits, long cable runs in commercial buildings).

The cost premium of fiber over passive copper HDMI is significant but justified for long runs where the alternative is an HDBaseT extender system. For runs under 30 meters, active copper cables are typically more cost-effective.

## Compatibility Verification

All extender technology has potential compatibility issues. HDCP handshake failures (the display shows a black screen or a "HDCP not supported" message), EDID mismatches (the source outputs the wrong resolution because it cannot read the display's EDID through the extender), and USB enumeration failures (the computer does not see the camera through the extender) are the most common problems.

Extender manufacturers publish compatibility matrices — lists of source and display combinations that have been tested and verified. Check the compatibility matrix for your specific compute unit and display combination before specifying an extender. If the combination is not on the matrix, test it during staging before committing it to the installation.

When an HDBaseT run shows video but the resolution is incorrect, EDID passing is the likely issue. Many HDBaseT transmitters have EDID management features that allow the transmitter to learn and store the display's EDID and present it to the source regardless of what the receiver reports — this is the standard fix for EDID problems in HDBaseT installations.

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **State passive limits:** HDMI passive ~10–15m; active HDMI ~20–30m; USB 3.x passive ~2–3m; USB 2.0 passive ~5m; Cat6A 100m; DP 1.4 passive ~3m.
- **Select extension technology:** HDBaseT for display runs over 30m (one Cat6A cable, up to 100m); USB over CAT for camera USB runs over 5–10m; fiber AOC for runs over 30m requiring ground isolation; active cables for medium-distance extensions.
- **Plan for extender requirements:** Power at the receiver, compatibility verification during staging, EDID management for HDBaseT.
