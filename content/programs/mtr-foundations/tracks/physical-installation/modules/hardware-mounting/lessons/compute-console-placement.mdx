## Overview

The touch console is the primary user interface for an MTR room — where participants join meetings, control volume, share content, and end calls. Where it sits and how its cable is managed are decisions that significantly affect the user experience. The compute unit is the brain of the system — where it lives determines how reliably it operates, how easy it is to service, and how secure it is from unauthorized access. Both placements require thought before hardware arrives on-site.

---

## Touch Console Placement

**Position on the table:** Microsoft's MTR design guidance places the touch console on the conference table or on a credenza at the table's end, within arm's reach of seated participants. For tables up to approximately 12 feet in length, a single console centered on the table serves all seats adequately. For tables over 14 feet, two consoles — one at each end — are recommended so participants at both ends of the table can reach a console without leaning across the table.

**ADA accessibility:** The Americans with Disabilities Act Standards for Accessible Design (Section 308, Reach Ranges) specifies that tabletop controls must be reachable without requiring a forward reach over an obstruction greater than 10 inches. In practice, this means the touch console must be within approximately 24 inches of the near edge of the table. Placing the console in the center of a 48-inch wide table is typically compliant — placing it at the far end of the table from where participants approach is not.

**Cable concealment:** The console's cable run (USB for USB-connected consoles, Ethernet for IP-based consoles like Logitech Tap IP) must be concealed. The professional standard for conference room console cabling: route through a table grommet to the inside of the table leg or credenza, then through furniture to the compute unit location. Exposed cables lying across the table surface or draped to the floor are unacceptable in a professional commercial installation and will generate client complaints immediately.

If the table does not have a grommet at the correct position, a surface-mounted grommet can be installed by drilling through the table at the desired location. Verify the table material (wood, laminate, glass, stone) and confirm drilling is appropriate before modifying client furniture. When table modification is not possible, a cable raceway routed along the table edge or a furniture channel product is the alternative.

## Compute Unit Placement

The compute unit (e.g., Lenovo ThinkSmart Core, Crestron UC-MX70, Neat Bar) must be located in a position that satisfies four requirements simultaneously:

**Ventilation:** Every compute unit specification sheet lists operating temperature requirements (typically 0–40°C ambient) and minimum clearance distances for airflow (typically 2–4 inches on intake and exhaust sides). In a credenza, the compute unit must have airflow path — either through grilles in the credenza doors, active ventilation fans, or an open-back credenza design. A sealed credenza with a fully loaded compute unit will exceed thermal limits within hours of operation, causing performance throttling and reduced hardware life.

**Maintenance access:** A technician must be able to reach the compute unit without disassembling furniture, removing the display, or requiring specialized tools. The compute unit should be accessible for USB device connection (for admin keyboard/mouse during commissioning and support), power cycling, and replacement. In a credenza, "accessible" means opening the credenza door provides direct access. Behind a locked panel or inside a sealed furniture void does not constitute maintainable access.

**Cable management:** The compute unit should be located as close as practical to the cable infrastructure termination point — the location where network, display, camera, and audio cables converge. Every foot of excess cable between the compute unit and its cable connections is cable that must be managed somewhere. Placing the compute unit near the infrastructure reduces cable clutter and makes cable management cleaner.

**Physical security:** The compute unit should not be accessible to general room occupants without authorization. Unauthorized USB device insertion (a key attack vector for security-conscious clients) and inadvertent power cycling are risks if the compute unit is visible and accessible on an open shelf. Inside a credenza with a lock, in a rack with a door, or behind a locked panel are appropriate locations for the compute unit in security-conscious environments. Discuss physical security requirements with the client during design — some clients require it, some do not, and this affects where the compute unit can be placed.

## Integrated Bundle Placement

Many current MTR solutions are integrated bundles — the compute, audio, and sometimes camera are consolidated into a single or dual-unit assembly (e.g., Poly Studio X, Neat Bar Pro, Logitech Rally Bar). For these systems, placement is determined by the OEM installation guide, which specifies the allowed positions, ventilation requirements, and cable routing. Follow the OEM guide precisely — integrated bundles are designed for specific mounting configurations and field improvisation on placement creates thermal and cable management problems that are difficult to resolve after installation.

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **Position the touch console:** Centered on the table (or two consoles for tables over 14 feet), within ADA reach range (~24 inches from table edge), cable concealed through grommet and furniture.
- **Place the compute unit:** With ventilation clearance per OEM spec, accessible for maintenance without furniture disassembly, near the infrastructure termination point, and physically secured from unauthorized access.
- **Document placement:** As-built drawings show the actual position of compute unit and console relative to furniture and infrastructure — not just the design intent.
