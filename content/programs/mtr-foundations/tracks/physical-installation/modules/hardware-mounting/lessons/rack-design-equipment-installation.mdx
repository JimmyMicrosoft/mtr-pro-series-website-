## Overview

A professional AV rack is planned before anything is installed. The rack elevation drawing — the to-scale top-to-bottom layout of every piece of equipment, every blank panel, and every cable management accessory — is produced at the design stage and used as the installation guide. Racks built without elevation planning become disorganized, overheat, and are difficult to service. Racks built from a considered elevation drawing look professional, manage heat correctly, and can be serviced by any technician who understands the drawing.

---

## Rack Unit Planning and Elevation Drawing

A standard EIA 310-D equipment rack uses 1.75-inch rack units (1U) on 19-inch wide equipment rails. Before any equipment is installed, produce a rack elevation drawing showing the full rack, top to bottom, with every item in its assigned position. Elevation drawings are typically produced in specialized AV design software (CAD, Visio, D-Tools) or on paper for simple racks.

**Layout sequence principles:**

- **Heavier equipment in the lower half of the rack:** A rack's center of gravity must stay low. Amplifiers, heavy power equipment, and multi-unit devices go below the midpoint. Lightweight DSPs, compute units, and wall controllers go in the upper half.

- **Cable management panels adjacent to connection-dense equipment:** A 1U horizontal cable management panel above and below the patch panel keeps patch cables organized. A panel adjacent to the switch keeps network cables dressed into the vertical management path.

- **Blank panels in all unused rack spaces:** Open rack spaces create short-circuit airflow — cool air that should be drawn through equipment instead passes unobstructed through the open space, reducing the airflow through equipment intakes. Fill every unused U with a blank panel. This is a thermal requirement, not cosmetic.

## Thermal Management

Heat rises. Rack thermal management uses this physics deliberately:

- **Heat-generating equipment (amplifiers, compute units with high processing loads) near the top** of the equipment zone so hot exhaust exits upward and does not recirculate through equipment below.
- **Fan trays or ventilated panels at the top** of the rack to exhaust accumulated hot air.
- **Blank panels in open spaces** (as noted above) to force airflow through equipment.

For enclosed racks (with solid doors, front and rear) in IDF or rack rooms, passive ventilation is insufficient for fully loaded systems. Active rack cooling — rack fan trays, rear-door heat exchangers, or supplemental room HVAC — is required. Calculate the total rack heat dissipation in BTU/hr (1W = 3.41 BTU/hr; sum all equipment wattages) and verify that the room cooling capacity can handle the load plus the heat contribution of other equipment in the room.

For credenza-mounted systems (small, shallow racks or open-shelf installations in furniture), ensure the credenza has ventilation openings. A small credenza fan pulling air out of the enclosure is often the difference between a compute unit that operates at 35°C and one that operates at 55°C.

## Power Distribution Units

Every rack should have a rack-mounted PDU (Power Distribution Unit) providing power to all rack equipment. For MTR systems, use a switched and metered PDU with per-outlet control — this enables remote power cycling of individual devices without sending a technician on-site. The network-accessible PDU becomes the first response tool for power-related remote support calls.

Position the PDU in the vertical channel of the rack (zero-U mounting on the side rail) or in a dedicated horizontal 1U PDU position at the bottom of the rack. Route PDU power cords to each device separately — no device should share a PDU outlet with another device (power cycling one device should not affect others). Separate power cables from signal cables: PDU and power cords on one side of the rack, signal cables (network, audio, video, control) on the other.

---

## Cable Dressing in Racks

Rack cable dressing follows from the principles established in Module 2.2:

- **Dress cables before terminating at rear panel connections.** Route from the rack entry point to the termination point, leaving planned slack coiled at horizontal management panels, before making the final connection.
- **Hook-and-loop straps, not cable ties.** Every bundle should be manageable after installation — additions and replacements happen throughout the rack's service life.
- **Grommets and bushings at all cable entry points.** Every location where cables enter the rack frame (top, bottom, sides) must have a bushing or grommet to prevent cable jacket abrasion from the sharp metal rack edges.
- **Power on one side, signal on the other.** Maintain this separation at every vertical management accessory and horizontal management panel.

---

## Equipment Installation Procedure

Physical installation of rack-mounted equipment follows a specific sequence:

1. **Install rear rail support or equipment shelves first** for any equipment that requires it (devices heavier than approximately 10 lbs or deeper than 1U should not be supported only by front rack ears).
2. **Install equipment in bottom-to-top order** so the rack remains balanced during installation (heaviest items go in first, at the bottom).
3. **Torque all rack screws appropriately** — under-torqued screws back out over time; over-torqued screws strip the rack nut threads. Use a calibrated torque screwdriver for rack screw applications in critical installations.
4. **Dress cables immediately after each equipment connection** — do not wait until all equipment is installed to begin dressing. Cable management is much harder retroactively.
5. **Apply all labels before commissioning** — label all rack equipment, all cables at both ends, and all patch panel ports before the first power-on.

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **Design a rack elevation:** Heavy equipment bottom half; blank panels in all empty spaces; cable management panels adjacent to connection-dense equipment; PDU in vertical channel.
- **Manage rack thermals:** Hot-producing equipment positioned so exhaust exits upward; blank panels force airflow through equipment; active cooling required for enclosed racks under full load.
- **Dress and label the rack:** Hook-and-loop straps; power and signal on opposite sides; grommets at all cable entry points; labels before first power-on.
- **Install in sequence:** Rear support before front ears; bottom to top; dress immediately after each connection.
