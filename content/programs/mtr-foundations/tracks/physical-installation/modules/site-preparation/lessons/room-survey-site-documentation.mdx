## Overview

The room survey is the integrator's primary risk mitigation tool. Assumptions made without a survey are the root cause of the majority of MTR project cost overruns and installation delays. A confident pre-installation survey turns unknowns into documented facts — room dimensions, wall construction, infrastructure locations, cable pathways, and conditions that require special handling all become part of a package that drives accurate design, accurate bills of materials, and accurate scheduling. The integrator who arrives on installation day with a complete survey package rarely gets surprised. The integrator who shows up without one regularly does.

This lesson covers what to measure, what to photograph, what to assess, and how to organize the results into a professional site documentation package.

---

## What the Room Survey Captures

A complete room survey for an MTR deployment captures six categories of information. Each category feeds a different part of the system design or installation plan.

**Room Dimensions and Geometry:** Measure and record the room's length, width, and ceiling height. Ceiling height determines camera mounting options — a ceiling mount typically requires 8–12 feet for adequate downward camera angle to frame seated participants. Room length and width feed the display sizing calculation (DISCAS, covered below) and the microphone coverage design. Note any architectural features that affect sightlines or cable routing: columns, beams, soffits, stepped ceilings, raised floors.

**Wall Construction Type:** Identify the wall construction at the intended display mounting location, camera mounting locations, and cable penetration points. Drywall on metal stud (standard commercial construction) is straightforward for cable routing and display mounting with proper backing. Masonry or concrete walls require specialized anchors, potentially surface-mounted raceways, and significantly more installation time. Glass partitions and curtain wall systems require custom mounting solutions. Fire-rated walls require rated cable and firestopping at all penetrations — this is a code requirement, not an option.

**Existing Infrastructure Inventory:** Document all existing conduit pathways, cable trays, J-hooks, pull boxes, and cable penetrations. Record the location of the nearest network closet or IDF. Note existing power outlets, circuit locations, and any existing UPS. Identify HVAC diffuser and vent locations — these affect microphone placement and ambient noise levels. An existing pathway to the IDF is valuable; a room with no usable pathways requires new conduit or surface raceway, which changes the installation scope significantly.

**Network and Power Points:** Record the exact location of every existing network jack and power outlet in the room. Identify which are usable for the MTR system versus occupied by existing furniture or equipment. Note the outlet type (standard 15A duplex, 20A, etc.) and whether dedicated circuits are available.

**Furniture and Layout:** Document the existing furniture arrangement — table dimensions and position, seating layout, credenza locations. Note whether furniture is fixed or movable. Display placement, camera positioning, touch console location, and cable routing all depend on the furniture arrangement the client intends to use.

**Conditions of Concern:** Flag anything that would affect installation cost, timeline, or approach: asbestos-containing materials (requires certified abatement before any penetrations), water damage, existing cable that needs to be traced or removed, structural anomalies, accessibility constraints, or evidence of previous failed installations.

## DISCAS Display Sizing

During the room survey, you collect the measurements needed to specify the correct display size for the room. AVIXA's DISCAS standard (Display Image Size for 2D Content in Audio-Visual Systems) provides the formula.

The key measurement is the viewing distance — the distance from the display face to the farthest seated viewer. DISCAS then specifies minimum image height (H) as a fraction of that distance (D), based on content type:

- **Decision-making content** (spreadsheets, detailed data): Minimum H = D/6, Optimal H = D/4
- **Passive content** (presentations, slides): Minimum H = D/8

Once you have the required image height, convert to diagonal display size for the room's aspect ratio. For a 16:9 display: diagonal = H × 2.04 (approximately).

**Example:** A 20-foot conference room with the display at one end places the farthest viewer at approximately 18 feet. For spreadsheet content at decision-making quality: minimum H = 18 ÷ 6 = 3 feet (36 inches). Diagonal = 36 × 2.04 = 73.4 inches. Specify the next available size up — an 86-inch display would be the appropriate recommendation.

Document the DISCAS calculation in the survey package alongside the room measurements. This gives you a defensible basis for the display size you specify — clients who push back on display cost have a harder time arguing against a calculation derived from their own room dimensions.

## Photographic Documentation

Photographs are time-stamped evidence of pre-existing conditions and the fastest way to communicate site conditions to team members who were not present for the survey. A minimum photographic set for an MTR room survey includes:

All four walls (full-width photos from the center of the room), the ceiling from four positions, existing infrastructure access points (conduit penetrations, cable trays, J-hook locations), the network closet or IDF (showing the patch panel, switch, and available port capacity), each existing power outlet and network jack, the area behind where the display will be mounted (to document backing requirements), and any conditions of concern flagged during the walkthrough.

Use a phone or camera that embeds GPS and timestamp metadata in photos. Organize photos by room and by category. A photo of a problem condition is worth more than a note in a survey form when explaining to a client why a scope addition is required.

## Site Documentation Package

The room survey output is a site documentation package that becomes part of the project record. A professional package includes:

- Dimensioned room sketch (hand-drawn or CAD, to scale or with measured dimensions annotated)
- Infrastructure inventory checklist
- Photographic record organized by subject
- DISCAS display sizing calculation
- Conditions of concern summary with photos
- Survey date, surveyor name, and client representative present

This package is shared with the design team (or used directly if the integrator is also the designer), informs the bill of materials, and is retained as project documentation. For multi-room or multi-site projects, it is typically stored in the project management system and referenced throughout the project lifecycle.

<Callout type="tip">
  On your first survey for a new client, take more photos than you think you need. You
  will almost always want to refer back to a photo you almost skipped. Storage is cheap;
  a return site visit to photograph something you missed is not.
</Callout>

## Summary

By completing this lesson, you can now:

- **Conduct a complete room survey:** Six categories — dimensions, wall construction, existing infrastructure, network/power, furniture, and conditions of concern.
- **Apply DISCAS for display sizing:** Decision-making content = D/6 minimum image height; passive content = D/8. Document the calculation in the survey package.
- **Produce a professional documentation package:** Dimensioned sketch, infrastructure inventory, photographic record, conditions summary.
- **Identify installation-altering conditions:** Masonry walls, HVAC constraints, fire-rated assemblies, asbestos, and existing infrastructure gaps all affect installation scope and must be documented before design is finalized.
