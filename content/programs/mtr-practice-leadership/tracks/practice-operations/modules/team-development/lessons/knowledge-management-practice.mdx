## The Knowledge Management Problem

Every AV/IT practice has this problem: one engineer knows how a client's unusual network setup works. One engineer knows the workaround for the firmware version on that specific camera model. One engineer has the undocumented configuration history of the 50-room client that's been on managed service for three years.

When that engineer takes annual leave, everything stops. When they resign, institutional knowledge walks out the door. When they are ill, the team is blind.

Knowledge management is the systematic process of converting individual knowledge into shared, documented, accessible knowledge. It is one of the most important operational investments a practice can make.

---

## Knowledge Categories

### Client Knowledge

Everything specific to a managed client that an engineer needs to provide effective support:

- Technical environment (network topology, VLAN assignments, switch models, firewall platform)
- M365 tenant configuration (tenant type, CA policies, hybrid setup, known exceptions)
- History of significant incidents (what happened, what fixed it, what to do differently)
- Key contacts (IT lead, network team, facilities, security — with direct contact details)
- Configuration deviations from standard (anything that differs from the design pattern)
- Access information (site access procedures, out-of-hours contacts, security requirements)

**Where to store**: Client record in ITSM (the single source of truth for client operational data).

### Technical Knowledge

General technical knowledge that the whole team benefits from:

- Hardware compatibility matrix (which USB peripherals are tested with which compute units)
- Common fault patterns and resolutions (what causes firmware X to fail; the AEC config that fixes echo in rooms with low ceilings)
- Integration notes (Biamp Tesira configuration for Logitech compute; Crestron integration gotchas)
- Microsoft update impact notes (what changed in Teams Rooms App version X; what broke in Windows update KB123456)

**Where to store**: Internal wiki or knowledge base (SharePoint, Confluence, or Notion).

### Process Knowledge

How the practice does things — procedures, standards, templates:

- Standard operating procedures (SOPs) for managed service (covered in service delivery module)
- Design pattern library and room specifications
- BOM and costing templates
- Proposal and SOW templates
- Quality checklists and acceptance test templates

**Where to store**: Document management system (SharePoint, Teams, or equivalent).

## Knowledge Management System Design

### The Three-Layer Architecture

**Layer 1: ITSM Client Records** (operational knowledge)
Every managed client has a complete, current client record in the ITSM containing all the operational information listed above. This is maintained by the service desk and updated on every significant change or incident.

**Layer 2: Knowledge Base** (technical knowledge)
A searchable knowledge base (SharePoint wiki, Confluence, or Notion) containing technical articles, integration notes, firmware compatibility, and troubleshooting guides. Updated when a new technique is discovered or a new issue is resolved.

**Layer 3: Document Management** (process knowledge)
A document library containing all templates, SOPs, design standards, and process documentation. Version controlled.

## Building a Documentation Culture

The hardest part of knowledge management is not the system — it is getting engineers to document what they know. Documentation feels like overhead; engineers prefer to solve the next problem.

**Strategies that work**:

1. **Make documentation part of ticket closure**: Require a knowledge article to be created or updated for any ticket that involved a novel fault or resolution. If the SOP was followed and it worked, close the ticket. If a new technique was needed, write it up before closing.

2. **Template everything**: Make the cost of documentation low by providing templates. "Fill in this template" is much easier than "write documentation."

3. **Make documentation findable**: A knowledge base no one can search is useless. Use tags and categories; make the search function prominent; link from ITSM tickets to relevant articles.

4. **Reward documentation**: Acknowledge engineers who contribute high-quality articles. Recognise the engineer whose article solved a problem for a colleague they'd never met.

5. **Model the behaviour**: If the practice manager doesn't document, no one will. The most powerful cultural signal is seeing leadership write and maintain documentation.

## Onboarding Using the Knowledge Base

A well-structured knowledge base should accelerate new engineer onboarding from 3 months to 6 weeks.

Onboarding plan structure:
- Week 1: Practice overview, client portfolio, tools access (ITSM, Pro Management, Intune)
- Week 2–3: Shadow senior engineer on service desk (supervised ticket response)
- Week 4–5: Supervised client interaction; first independent tickets with senior engineer review
- Week 6: First independent site visit with senior engineer debrief

The knowledge base makes Week 1–3 self-service. The new engineer can read client records, design standards, and SOPs before touching any live system. This reduces the senior engineer mentoring time required and gets the new team member productive faster.

## Knowledge Retention During Staff Transitions

When an engineer gives notice:

1. **Identify knowledge gaps immediately**: What does this engineer know that isn't documented?
2. **Schedule knowledge transfer sessions**: Formal sessions where the departing engineer walks through key clients, unusual configurations, and undocumented techniques
3. **Document while knowledge is fresh**: The departing engineer writes up or dictates; a colleague structures and publishes
4. **Client notification if appropriate**: For long-standing managed service clients, an account manager communication noting the transition and introducing the new primary contact

The minimum acceptable knowledge transfer period is 2 weeks for a service desk engineer; 4 weeks for a senior engineer or solutions architect.
