## Why Technology Deployments Fail to Deliver Value

The majority of technology deployment failures are not technical failures. The technology works. The rooms are installed. The devices are commissioned. But six months later, the rooms sit empty because people didn't change how they work.

Change management is the discipline of managing the human and organisational transition from current state to desired state. For MTR deployments, the desired state is not "rooms with Teams Rooms devices in them" — it is "a workforce that meets differently, more effectively, with better outcomes for remote and in-room participants."

---

## The Change Equation

Effective organisational change requires three elements to outweigh resistance:

```
(Vision × Dissatisfaction × First Steps) > Resistance to Change
```

**Vision**: People need to understand what the new world looks like and why it is better than today.

**Dissatisfaction**: People need to feel genuine pain with the current state. If the old meeting rooms work well enough, there's no motivation to change.

**First Steps**: People need to know exactly what to do next — not "use the new rooms" but "next time you book a meeting in Room 2A, here's exactly how to start it."

**Resistance**: Every organisation has resistance — technical (the system is too complicated), habitual (we've always done it this way), political (no one asked us), and comfort (I don't want to look incompetent in front of colleagues).

The project manager's change management role is to maximise each element on the left and minimise resistance on the right.

## Stakeholder Engagement

### Executive Sponsorship

The most powerful adoption driver is visible executive sponsorship. When the CFO uses the Teams Rooms device in the boardroom and talks about it, everyone pays attention.

**Actions to secure executive sponsorship:**
- Brief the executive sponsor at kickoff: what are you deploying, why it matters to the business, and what you need from them
- Identify 2–3 "sponsor behaviours" — specific visible actions the sponsor can take (using the room for their next all-hands, mentioning the rollout in a company update)
- Include the sponsor in the pilot launch — invite them to the first day the rooms go live

### Middle Management

Middle managers determine whether their teams use the new rooms or revert to old habits.

**Actions to engage middle managers:**
- Briefing before rooms go live (not an IT briefing — a business briefing about what the rooms enable)
- Ask managers to nominate floor champions and actively support them
- Share early adoption metrics with managers — peer comparison is a powerful motivator

### End Users

**Actions to engage end users:**
- Communicate before the rooms go live — "Your meeting rooms are changing. Here's what to expect and why."
- Address the WIIFM (What's In It For Me): hybrid meeting equity, better calls with remote colleagues, less technical frustration
- Make the first experience positive — ensure training happens before or at go-live, not weeks after

## Communication Planning

A communication plan maps messages to audiences and channels throughout the deployment:

| Phase | Timing | Audience | Message | Channel |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Awareness | 4 weeks before go-live | All staff | "We're upgrading the meeting rooms" | Email from sponsor |
| Preparation | 2 weeks before go-live | Affected floors | "Your rooms go live on [date]" | Team channel, intranet |
| Training | 1 week before go-live | All staff on affected floors | "Training available — sign up here" | Email, calendar invite |
| Launch | Go-live day | All staff | "The new rooms are live — here's how to use them" | Email from sponsor, intranet |
| Reinforcement | 30 days post go-live | Champions, IT | "How are the rooms working? Usage data update" | Champion network, IT meeting |

### Message Framing

Frame the change in terms of outcomes, not features:

| Don't say | Say instead |
|---|---|
| "We're deploying Teams Rooms devices" | "We're making hybrid meetings work better" |
| "You'll need to use the new touch console" | "You can join your next meeting with one tap" |
| "IT has deployed a new AV solution" | "Your remote colleagues will be able to see and hear you clearly for the first time" |

## Resistance Patterns and Responses

### "The old system was fine"

**Response**: Acknowledge the sentiment, then surface the specific pain it didn't solve. "I know the old system worked for in-room meetings. This is about making it work equally well for the people joining remotely. Have you ever been on a Teams call where you couldn't hear the room clearly?" If there is genuinely no pain with the current state, this may indicate the deployment was not driven by real user need — raise this with the project sponsor.

### "It's too complicated"

**Response**: Simplify. If users find the system complicated, the training was insufficient or the UI was not configured optimally. Revisit the quick reference card, simplify the default display layout, and schedule additional walkthrough sessions. The system should be demonstrably simpler for the most common use case (joining a scheduled meeting) than whatever it replaced.

### "I don't want to look stupid in front of my team"

**Response**: This is the most common unspoken resistance. The solution is low-stakes practice. Champions can run "drop-in" sessions where people can try the room with no meeting to join and no audience. Early access for teams before the broader go-live creates familiarity.

### "The rooms never work properly anyway"

**Response**: If this is based on past experience with the old system, it requires a demonstrated contrast with the new system in a live scenario. If it is based on early problems with the new system, it requires immediate technical resolution — every early failure reinforces the narrative and is extremely hard to reverse.

<Callout type="warning">
**A technically broken room that is visible to users in the first two weeks does lasting adoption damage.** Prioritise fixing reported issues immediately post go-live. Users who have one bad experience in the first fortnight often don't return for 6 months.
</Callout>

## Change Management Maturity by Client

Not every client needs the same change management investment. Assess maturity:

| Client Type | Change Management Need | Recommended Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Tech-forward; strong IT culture | Low | QR card, intranet video, champions programme |
| Traditional enterprise; change-resistant culture | High | Executive communication campaign, floor-by-floor launch events, active champion network, 30/60/90-day adoption reviews |
| Professional services; high meeting frequency | Medium | Emphasis on hybrid equity benefits; focus on client-facing meeting quality |
| Public sector; complex governance | Medium-High | Change authority approvals; structured comms through official channels |
