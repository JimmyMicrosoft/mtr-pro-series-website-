## Why MTR Projects Need Active Risk Management

MTR deployments involve dependencies across multiple client teams (IT, facilities, procurement, end users), multiple vendors (hardware, software, network), and physical installation constraints that cannot always be verified in advance. A risk that is not identified and managed will eventually materialise as a schedule delay, a budget overrun, or a failed acceptance.

Risk management is not about pessimism — it is about converting unknowns into knowns early enough to influence the outcome.

---

## The Risk Register

The risk register is a living document maintained throughout the project lifecycle. It is not a one-time deliverable created at scoping and filed away — it is reviewed at every project status meeting.

### Risk Register Structure

Each risk entry contains:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Risk ID | Sequential reference (R-001, R-002...) |
| Risk Description | Clear statement of what could go wrong |
| Category | Technical / Commercial / Organisational / External |
| Probability | 1–5 scale (1 = Rare, 5 = Almost Certain) |
| Impact | 1–5 scale (1 = Negligible, 5 = Critical) |
| Risk Score | Probability × Impact (1–25) |
| Priority | High (15–25) / Medium (8–14) / Low (1–7) |
| Mitigation Action | What we do to reduce likelihood |
| Contingency Response | What we do if the risk materialises |
| Owner | Named individual responsible for monitoring |
| Status | Open / Mitigated / Closed / Materialised |
| Review Date | Next scheduled review |

### Probability/Impact Scoring

**Probability scale:**
- 1 – Rare: Less than 10% chance
- 2 – Unlikely: 10–30% chance
- 3 – Possible: 30–60% chance
- 4 – Likely: 60–80% chance
- 5 – Almost Certain: Greater than 80% chance

**Impact scale:**
- 1 – Negligible: No meaningful impact on schedule, cost, or quality
- 2 – Minor: Up to 1-week delay or &lt;5% cost impact
- 3 – Moderate: 1–3 week delay or 5–15% cost impact
- 4 – Major: 3–8 week delay or 15–30% cost impact
- 5 – Critical: Project failure, significant financial loss, or client relationship damage

## Common MTR Project Risks

### Technical Risks

| Risk | Typical Score | Mitigation | Contingency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Network not ready at installation | High (4×4=16) | Complete NRA 4 weeks before install; include network readiness gate in plan | Delay install dates; engage client escalation path |
| Resource accounts not provisioned on time | High (4×3=12) | Provide provisioning guide 3 weeks ahead; confirm completion 5 days before | Defer commissioning; provision accounts on-site if admin access available |
| Ceiling void inaccessible for cable routing | Medium (3×3=9) | Verify void access during survey; include cable tray as alternative | Commission cable tray contractor; raise change request for additional works |
| SSL inspection blocking Teams auth | High (3×5=15) | Test during NRA; document firewall exemption requirement in SOW | Engage client network team; delay commissioning until resolved |
| Legacy display not HDMI-compatible | Low (2×2=4) | Inventory existing equipment during survey | Supply HDMI adapter; raise change request if display replacement needed |
| MTR app update changes behaviour mid-project | Medium (2×3=6) | Pin app version in Intune for duration of project | Test on pilot room before fleet rollout |

### Commercial Risks

| Risk | Typical Score | Mitigation | Contingency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Client adds rooms mid-project without change request | High (4×3=12) | Strong SOW with change control clause; brief client stakeholders at kickoff | Issue change request immediately when additional rooms are requested |
| Hardware delivery delay | Medium (3×4=12) | Order hardware minimum 6 weeks before install; confirm lead times | Source alternative devices; stagger installation schedule |
| Client unable to pay within terms | Low (2×4=8) | Agree payment milestones in contract; invoice on delivery | Pause additional phases; escalate to client finance |
| Scope dispute on exclusions | Medium (3×3=9) | Explicit exclusions in SOW; client sign-off before works begin | Refer to SOW; raise change request; escalate if unresolved |

### Organisational Risks

| Risk | Typical Score | Mitigation | Contingency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Client IT contact leaves or changes mid-project | Medium (3×3=9) | Identify a secondary contact at kickoff; document all knowledge transfers | Re-brief new contact; adjust schedule if knowledge gap significant |
| Rooms unavailable during installation window | High (4×3=12) | Confirm room bookings 2 weeks before install; daily confirmation day before | Resequence installation; reschedule unavailable rooms |
| End-user resistance to new system | Medium (3×2=6) | Include change management and training in scope; brief floor manager | Additional training sessions; escalate to executive sponsor |
| Client procurement delays hardware order | High (4×3=12) | Agree procurement process at kickoff; offer to manage procurement | Extend project timeline; replan installation dates |

## Risk Register in Practice

### At Scoping

Populate the risk register with all risks identified during the site survey and IT discovery. Assign initial probability and impact scores. Identify owners.

Present the register to the client at the project kickoff meeting — not as a list of problems, but as a demonstration of rigorous planning. Frame it as: "Here are the things we're watching to protect your project. These are the actions we're taking. These are the things we need from you."

### At Project Status Meetings

Review the risk register at every status meeting (recommended weekly for active projects):

1. Review all Open High-priority risks — confirm mitigation actions are on track
2. Check upcoming project milestones against risk triggers
3. Add any new risks identified since last review
4. Update status of risks that have materialised or been resolved
5. Confirm owner acknowledgement of assigned risks

### When a Risk Materialises

When a risk converts from "possible" to "actual":

1. Move status to "Materialised" in the register
2. Activate the contingency response
3. Notify the client sponsor immediately (do not wait for the next scheduled meeting)
4. Assess downstream impact on schedule, cost, and other risks
5. Update the project plan with revised dates

<Callout type="warning">
**Never surprise a client sponsor with a materialised risk in a project meeting.** Sponsors need a call or direct communication before any formal meeting where the issue will be discussed. Discovering a schedule-impacting problem in a group meeting — without prior notice — damages the relationship regardless of whose fault the risk was.
</Callout>

## Risk Appetite and Escalation

Different clients and projects have different risk tolerances. At project initiation, agree:

- What risk score threshold triggers escalation to the project sponsor?
- Who has authority to approve contingency actions without sponsor involvement?
- Are any risk categories (e.g., data security, building works) subject to special escalation rules?

Document these agreements in the project initiation document (PID) or project charter. This prevents ambiguity when a risk materialises under time pressure.
