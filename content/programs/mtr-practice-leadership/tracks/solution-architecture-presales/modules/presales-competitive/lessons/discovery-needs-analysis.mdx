## Discovery as the Foundation of the Sale

The quality of a proposal is determined by the quality of the discovery that precedes it. A proposal written without genuine discovery is a guess — it may accidentally match the client's needs, but more often it addresses the wrong problem, at the wrong price, for the wrong audience.

Effective discovery is not a list of technical questions about room dimensions and network topology. It is a structured conversation about business outcomes, organisational challenges, and decision-making dynamics.

---

## The Three Types of Buyers

In a complex MTR sale, there are typically three distinct buyer roles:

### The Economic Buyer

**Who**: The person with budget authority. Often the CIO, CFO, Head of IT, or COO. Sometimes the CEO for small organisations.

**Cares about**: Total cost, ROI, risk, strategic alignment. Does not care about product features unless they translate to cost savings or strategic outcomes.

**Discovery focus**: "What is the business case for this investment? What outcomes do you need to demonstrate to justify it?"

### The Technical Buyer

**Who**: The person who will evaluate whether the solution is technically appropriate. IT Manager, Network Manager, AV Manager, IT Director.

**Cares about**: Integration with existing systems, management overhead, security and compliance, support and warranty, technical risk.

**Discovery focus**: "What does your current environment look like? What technical constraints will this solution need to work within?"

### The User Buyer

**Who**: The people who will use the rooms daily. Could be senior executives (boardroom), general staff (standard rooms), or specific teams (sales, finance).

**Cares about**: Ease of use, reliability, the experience of being in a call, not looking incompetent in front of colleagues.

**Discovery focus**: "What does a bad meeting look like today? What would a great meeting experience mean to your teams?"

<Callout type="tip">
**Never let a sale be driven exclusively by the technical buyer.** Technical buyers optimise for features and price. Economic buyers optimise for business outcomes. If only the IT manager is involved, the proposal will be evaluated on spec sheets. If the CIO is involved, it will be evaluated on business impact — a much better conversation.
</Callout>

## SPIN Selling Framework for MTR Discovery

SPIN Selling (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff) is a structured questioning methodology that surfaces the pain that motivates buying decisions.

### Situation Questions

Establish the current state. These should be researched where possible (don't ask what you can find on LinkedIn or their website).

- "How many meeting rooms do you currently have across your estate?"
- "What video conferencing platform does the organisation use today?"
- "What percentage of your workforce is now working hybrid or remote?"
- "Have you deployed any video conferencing hardware before? What system?"

### Problem Questions

Surface explicit challenges and frustrations.

- "What are the most common complaints you hear about the current meeting room experience?"
- "How often do meetings start late due to technology issues?"
- "Do your remote participants have trouble hearing or seeing in-room participants?"
- "Is it easy for users to share their laptop screen in a meeting?"
- "Do you have visibility of which rooms are being used and how often?"

### Implication Questions

These deepen the pain by exploring the consequences of the problem. This is where good salespeople distinguish themselves — most stop at Problem questions.

- "When meetings consistently start 5–10 minutes late, what does that cost you in productivity across the organisation?"
- "If your remote workers can't participate effectively in hybrid meetings, how does that affect their engagement and decision-making influence?"
- "If a client meeting fails due to AV issues, what is the commercial impact?"
- "If your IT team is spending significant time troubleshooting meeting room technology, what is not getting done?"

### Need-Payoff Questions

These position the solution by asking the client to articulate the value of solving the problem. The client arrives at the value proposition themselves — far more powerful than the salesperson stating it.

- "If meetings consistently started on time and worked first time, what would that be worth?"
- "If your remote team members had the same meeting experience as in-room participants, how would that change how you work?"
- "If your IT team could manage all your meeting rooms from a single portal and get alerts when something fails, how much time would that save per week?"

## Qualification Framework

Not every opportunity is worth pursuing. Qualify before investing significant proposal time using the MEDDIC framework:

| Letter | Element | Questions |
|---|---|---|
| M | Metrics | What measurable outcome is the client trying to achieve? (utilisation, cost per meeting, etc.) |
| E | Economic Buyer | Have you identified and met the economic buyer? Do they have approved budget? |
| D | Decision Criteria | What criteria will they use to evaluate solutions? |
| D | Decision Process | Who else is involved? What does the approval process look like? Timeline? |
| I | Identify Pain | Have you identified specific, quantified business pain that this solves? |
| C | Champion | Is there an internal sponsor advocating for this? |

**Disqualify if**:
- No identified economic buyer with budget
- Decision criteria are purely on price (commoditised opportunity)
- No clear pain — this is exploratory with no urgency
- Timeline is completely open-ended
- A competitor has a deep relationship and you have not found a way to differentiate

## Discovery Questions That Differentiate MTR

These questions surface requirements that play to MTR's specific strengths:

- "Does your organisation use Microsoft 365 for email, calendar, and Teams?" → If yes, MTR's M365 integration is a natural fit
- "Are you concerned about fragmented tooling — different platforms in different rooms?" → Consistency argument
- "Do you have an IT team that manages Windows devices with Intune or SCCM?" → MTR is just another Windows endpoint
- "Do you have security or compliance requirements that affect endpoint management?" → Intune + Conditional Access
- "Are there board members or senior executives whose meeting room experience directly reflects on the IT team?" → Premium experience argument
- "Is meeting room utilisation visible to you today? Do you know which rooms are underused?" → Pro Management analytics argument
