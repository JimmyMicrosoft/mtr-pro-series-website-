## The BOM as the Commercial Foundation

The bill of materials is the foundation of every project cost. An incomplete or inaccurate BOM produces an undercosted proposal — which either wins unprofitably or loses when the margin correction is applied.

BOM accuracy comes from:
1. A thorough site survey (you know exactly what each room needs)
2. Validated design patterns (you aren't guessing what components the design requires)
3. Discipline in including everything (cables, mounts, consumables, spares)

---

## BOM Structure

A complete MTR project BOM has four sections:

### Section 1: Hardware

All equipment to be supplied and installed:

```
Line | QTY | Manufacturer | Part Number   | Description                   | Buy Price | Sell Price | Extended Sell
-----|-----|-------------|---------------|------------------------------|-----------|------------|-------------
1    | 20  | Logitech    | 960-001390    | Rally Bar (Graphite)          | £1,800    | £2,340     | £46,800
2    | 20  | Samsung     | LH75QMBPAGCXEN| 75" QMB-T Commercial Display  | £680      | £884       | £17,680
3    | 20  | Samsung     | —             | VESA wall mount kit           | £45       | £58        | £1,160
4    | 20  | Logitech    | 952-000089    | Tap IP Touch Controller       | £420      | £546       | £10,920
5    | 20  | Generic     | —             | 3m HDMI 2.0 cable             | £12       | £16        | £320
6    | 20  | Generic     | —             | Cat6 patch cable (2m)         | £4        | £5         | £100
7    | 2   | Generic     | —             | Spare Tap IP (5% buffer)      | £420      | £420       | £840 [cost only]
...
```

**Margin approach**: Apply consistent markup to hardware (typically 15–25% depending on vendor and volume). Track buy price and sell price separately.

### Section 2: Software and Licensing

Annual recurring costs that the client should understand as ongoing OpEx:

```
Line | QTY | Item                          | Annual Cost | Notes
-----|-----|-------------------------------|-------------|------
1    | 20  | Teams Rooms Pro license        | £450/device | Client purchases direct from Microsoft; or through partner
2    | 1   | MTR Managed Service (if sold)  | £X/year     | Per separate MSA
```

**Note**: Reselling Microsoft licenses requires CSP partner status. Confirm whether the practice is a CSP partner or whether the client purchases direct.

### Section 3: Labour

All professional services hours:

```
Labour Category              | Rate  | Hours | Total
-----------------------------|-------|-------|------
Project management           | £85   | 40    | £3,400
Site survey and NRA (already done) | £85 | 16  | £1,360
Design and documentation     | £75   | 20    | £1,500
Installation (per room × 20) | £65   | 4 hrs/room = 80 hrs | £5,200
Commissioning (per room × 20)| £65   | 1.5 hrs/room = 30 hrs | £1,950
Acceptance testing           | £65   | 16    | £1,040
End-user training            | £65   | 8     | £520
Documentation (as-builts)    | £55   | 16    | £880
Travel (estimate)            | —     | —     | £600
Total labour:                |       |       | £16,450
```

Labour rates should reflect actual loaded cost (salary + NI + benefits + overhead allocation) with appropriate margin.

### Section 4: Contingency

Include an explicit contingency line in the cost model (not in the client price):

```
Project contingency: 10% of hardware + labour = £(total × 0.10)
```

This is an internal buffer — it does not appear in the client-facing proposal unless the practice's commercial model exposes it. It protects the project from minor scope variations and unknown site conditions.

## Common BOM Errors

These are the items most often missed in MTR BOM preparation:

### Physical

- **VESA mounts**: Every display needs a mount. Mount cost varies significantly (standard wall mount £45; heavy-duty articulating arm £180; ceiling mount £200+)
- **Cable lengths**: BOM cables are always shorter than actual route lengths. Add 20% to straight-line measurements for actual installed lengths
- **Cable containment**: Surface trunking, floor boxes, and conduit are rarely included in the design pattern BOM — add as a site-specific line item
- **Power supplies**: Some cameras and control systems require separate power adaptors — confirm what's in the box

### Technical

- **USB extenders or hubs**: Logitech Rally requires an active USB extension for compute-to-hub cable runs over 5m
- **Display control cables**: RS-232 or LAN control cable from compute to display (often 2–3m behind display)
- **HDMI ingest cable**: The table cable for laptop sharing. Specify length; add cable anchor/retractor
- **PoE switches**: If the existing switch doesn't have PoE+ ports, a PoE injector or switch upgrade may be needed

### Organisational

- **Spares**: For a fleet of 20+, hold 1–2 spare units of the most critical components (touch controller, compute unit)
- **Label materials**: Cable labels, asset tags — small cost but time-consuming to add as a change after delivery

## Margin Discipline

### Margin vs Markup

Margin and markup are different:
- **Markup**: Percentage added to cost → Sell price = Cost × (1 + markup%)
- **Margin**: Percentage of sell price that is profit → Margin = (Sell - Cost) / Sell

| Markup | Effective Margin |
|---|---|
| 10% | 9.1% |
| 20% | 16.7% |
| 25% | 20.0% |
| 33% | 24.8% |

Know which metric your practice uses for pricing targets and be consistent.

### Target Margins

| Revenue Type | Target Margin | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hardware supply | 15–25% | Depends on vendor rebates and volume |
| Installation labour | 35–45% | Loaded cost to sell rate |
| Project management | 40–50% | Senior resource; high value per hour |
| Managed service (recurring) | 50–65% | Highly leveraged; target grows with scale |

### Red Lines

- Never submit a proposal without knowing the total cost and total margin
- If a client-imposed discount brings margin below 15% on hardware, the deal is not worth taking unless there is a strategic reason (new logo, large managed service attached)
- Undercutting competitors on price to win is not a sustainable strategy — it conditions the client to expect low prices on every subsequent deal
