## The Proposal Presentation

Sending a proposal document and asking for feedback is a weak close. Presenting a proposal live — in person or by video — is a strong move. You control the narrative, you can respond to objections in real time, and you can read the room's reaction.

Always request a proposal presentation meeting unless the client explicitly declines.

---

## Preparation

### Know Your Audience

Before the presentation, confirm who will be in the room:

- **Technical buyer (IT Manager)**: Will probe on integration, management, security. Prepare specific technical answers.
- **Economic buyer (CIO/CFO)**: Will focus on total cost, ROI, risk, and vendor credibility. Prepare the financial summary.
- **User buyer (Facilities, Operations Manager)**: Will care about reliability, ease of use, and disruption during installation. Prepare deployment approach and training plan.

If all three are present, you need to speak to all three — often in sequence or alternating focus.

### Prepare Your Proposal Pack

Leave-behind materials for the presentation:
- Printed proposal (executive summary version — not the full technical document)
- Architecture diagram for each room type (one page, visual)
- Reference client one-pager (two to three client examples relevant to this audience)
- ROI/TCO summary on one page

### Anticipate the Top Three Objections

Based on the discovery conversation and the competitive situation, identify the three most likely objections and prepare responses:

1. Price is too high
2. Concern about deployment disruption
3. Preference for a competitor platform

## Presentation Structure (45–60 minutes)

### Opening (5 min): Confirm the Agenda and the Outcome

"Thank you for making time today. Our goal for this session is to walk you through how we'd approach your project, address any questions on the technical approach and commercial terms, and by the end of the meeting, understand whether we're the right partner for you to move forward with."

This framing is collaborative, not pushy. It also creates a natural close: "Based on what we've discussed today, are we aligned?" at the end.

### Discovery Recap (5 min)

"Before we get into the proposal, I want to confirm that we've understood your requirements correctly." Briefly restate the key pain points from discovery. Ask: "Have I captured your priorities correctly? Is there anything that has changed since we last spoke?"

This demonstrates listening, creates alignment, and surfaces any new requirements before you've spent 30 minutes presenting.

### Solution Walkthrough (20 min)

Walk through the proposal in order of relevance to this audience:

- For a technical audience: lead with the solution architecture and management approach
- For a commercial audience: lead with the outcomes, then the approach, then the numbers
- For a mixed audience: executive summary first (for the buyer), solution second (for the IT team)

Use the room design diagram. Show the room schedule. Reference the management portal.

### Commercial Summary (10 min)

Present the pricing options (Option A / B / C). Don't apologise for the price. Present it confidently with the value context:

"Across 20 rooms, Option C at £X/year represents under £200 per room per month for a fully managed, proactively monitored service with a 2-hour response SLA. Based on the meeting hours you expect these rooms to facilitate, that's well under £1 per meeting."

### Reference and Credibility (5 min)

Briefly reference 1–2 comparable client deployments. If you can arrange a reference call or site visit, offer it here.

### Q&A and Close (15 min)

Open the floor. Listen actively. Take notes. Do not fill silence — when a question is answered, wait.

After Q&A, close:

"Based on what we've covered today, I'd like to understand where you are in your decision. Are there any remaining questions or concerns before you're ready to move forward? Is there a timeline we should be aware of?"

If the client is ready to proceed: "Then the next step is for us to agree terms and issue a contract. Can we aim to have that signed by [date]?"

If the client needs more time: "What would help you reach a decision? A reference call? A site visit? A revised scope on one of the options?"

## Closing Techniques

### The Alternative Close

Offer two paths forward, both of which represent progress:

"Would you prefer to proceed with Option B and add the managed service at renewal, or is Option C the right starting point given your current IT capacity?"

The client answers by choosing between two options — not between "yes" and "no."

### The Timeline Close

Use the client's own timeline to create urgency:

"You mentioned you need the rooms ready for your new office opening in September. Given our installation schedule, we'd need to confirm the order by the end of May to guarantee that timeline. Is there anything preventing us from confirming by then?"

### The Summary Close

Summarise the agreements reached during the meeting:

"So we're aligned that the 20-room standard configuration is the right scope, Option B pricing works commercially, and the target go-live is Q3. The only remaining step is contract signature. Can we schedule that for this week?"

### The Reference Close

"Before you make your final decision, would it be helpful to speak to [reference client name] who did a similar deployment with us last year? They'd be happy to share their experience."

A reference offer signals confidence and gives hesitant clients a way to reduce their perceived risk.

---

## Post-Presentation Follow-Up

Within 24 hours of the presentation:
- Email summary of key points discussed and agreed
- Address any specific questions or information requests made during the meeting
- Confirm the agreed next steps and timeline
- Attach any additional materials requested

If no decision after 5 business days: a single follow-up email asking for status. If no response: one phone call. If still no response: acknowledge the silence gracefully and maintain the relationship — the client may return when the timing is right.
