## What a Good Proposal Does

A good proposal achieves three things:

1. **Confirms understanding**: Shows the client you understood their problem and their context
2. **Describes the solution**: Explains what you are proposing and why it is the right answer
3. **Justifies confidence**: Demonstrates why this practice is the right team to deliver it

A proposal is a communication document, not a technical specification. The technical specification lives in the architecture documentation (which may be appended). The proposal's job is to convince.

---

## Proposal Structure

### Section 1: Executive Summary (1–2 pages)

**Audience**: Sponsor, CIO, CFO. Often the only section they read.

Content:
- The client's problem or opportunity, stated in the client's own language (from discovery)
- The proposed solution in 2–3 sentences — no product names, no jargon
- The key outcomes the client will achieve
- A headline commercial summary (project value; managed service value if applicable)
- A call to action

**Example executive summary opening**:
"You told us that meeting technology failures are costing your teams time, damaging client relationships, and making remote employees feel like second-class participants. This proposal describes a Microsoft Teams Rooms deployment that resolves all three challenges — delivered by a team that has commissioned over 500 MTR devices across 30 enterprise clients in the last three years."

The executive summary should be written last (when you know exactly what you're proposing) but positioned first.

### Section 2: Understanding the Requirement (1 page)

Show you listened. Summarise the key requirements identified during discovery:
- Organisational context (who, what, why)
- Technical environment (M365, current AV, network)
- Business requirements (reliability, hybrid equity, management, growth plans)
- Constraints and dependencies (budget, timeline, IT resource, compliance)

Ending this section with: "Our proposal addresses each of these requirements" directly connects the discovery to the solution.

### Section 3: Proposed Solution (2–4 pages)

**3.1 Solution Overview**
Describe the solution architecture at a high level. Room types, hardware philosophy, management approach. Use diagrams where possible.

**3.2 Room Design**
Describe each room design pattern in plain language. Reference the attached Room Schedule and Architecture Documentation.

**3.3 Microsoft Teams Rooms Platform**
Briefly explain what MTR is, why it is the right platform for this environment (M365 integration, management, licensing), and what the client experience will be.

**3.4 Management and Support**
Describe how the fleet will be managed: Pro Management portal, Intune, update strategy. If including a managed service, outline the service tiers and SLA.

**3.5 Deployment Approach**
High-level delivery plan: site survey complete, hardware order, installation phasing, commissioning, training, handover.

### Section 4: About the Practice (1 page)

The client is buying a capability, not just a product. Evidence that your practice can deliver:
- How long you've been delivering MTR
- Volume of MTR devices deployed
- 2–3 reference client statements or case study summaries
- Microsoft partner status (Solutions Partner for Modern Work, etc.)
- Relevant certifications (Microsoft Certified: Teams Rooms Administrator, AVIXA CTS, etc.)

Keep this factual and specific. "We are a leading provider of..." is meaningless. "We have deployed 500+ MTR devices across 30 enterprise clients, including three FTSE 250 organisations" is specific and credible.

### Section 5: Commercial Summary

**5.1 Pricing Summary**
The client-facing cost breakdown. Do not show line-item buy prices:

```
Supply and installation (20 rooms):     £X
Project management and documentation:   £X
End-user training programme:            £X
Total project investment:               £X

Optional: Teams Rooms Pro licensing (20 × £450/year): £9,000/year
Optional: Managed Service (Level 2 — see Appendix B): £16,000/year
```

**5.2 Payment Terms**
Proposed milestone-based payments:
- 40% on order confirmation
- 40% on installation completion
- 20% on project acceptance

**5.3 Validity Period**
Pricing valid for 30 days from proposal date (protects against hardware price changes and labour availability).

### Section 6: Next Steps

Specific, actionable next steps that move the sale forward:
- "Please review and confirm acceptance of the technical scope by [date]"
- "We propose a 60-minute technical review session with your IT team to validate the design before contract signature"
- "If you'd like to visit a reference site before deciding, we can arrange this with [reference client] in [city]"

## Writing Principles

### Write for the buyer, not the seller

Every sentence should serve the reader's interests. Features are interesting to sellers; outcomes are interesting to buyers.

| Seller language | Buyer language |
|---|---|
| "Equipped with a Logitech Rally Bar with integrated 4K camera" | "Your remote participants will see every in-room attendee clearly from anywhere in the room" |
| "Managed via Microsoft Intune" | "Your IT team manages these devices through the same tools they already use for laptops — zero additional platform complexity" |
| "99.9% SLA uptime commitment" | "If a room fails at 3am, we know about it before your first user arrives at 8am" |

### Be specific, not vague

"Extensive experience" → "350+ MTR devices commissioned"
"Fast response times" → "P1 response in 2 hours, P2 in 4 hours"
"Industry leading" → delete; say something specific instead

### Eliminate jargon unless defined

If you use an acronym, define it on first use. Not everyone reading the proposal knows what MTR, QoS, VLAN, or Intune means. The IT manager does; the CFO and facilities manager may not.
